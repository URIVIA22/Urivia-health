GLP-1 and Kidney Labs: How to Read Your Results

By the UriVia Health team Last updated April 2026

If you've had kidney labs drawn since starting a GLP-1 medication and your numbers look slightly off, you're not alone. GLP-1 medications can temporarily shift certain kidney markers, usually because of changes in hydration rather than kidney damage. This post explains which labs matter, how GLP-1s affect them, how to read your own lab report, and what to ask your doctor before assuming something is wrong.

Note upfront: this post is educational. It does not interpret your specific numbers or tell you whether your results are concerning. Only your doctor can do that in the context of your full history.

Which labs matter (eGFR, creatinine, BUN, ACR)

Four lab values do most of the work in kidney function assessment.

eGFR (estimated glomerular filtration rate). This is the number most commonly cited as "kidney function." It estimates how much blood your kidneys filter per minute, adjusted for body size. A normal eGFR for most adults is above 60 mL/min/1.73m². Values below 60 sustained over three months meet the criteria for chronic kidney disease, according to the National Kidney Foundation. eGFR is calculated from serum creatinine, so the two always move together.

Serum creatinine. Creatinine is a waste product from muscle metabolism. Your kidneys filter it out of blood and into urine. When kidney function drops, creatinine in the blood rises. Normal ranges are roughly 0.6 to 1.2 mg/dL for adult men and 0.5 to 1.1 mg/dL for adult women, though labs report their own reference ranges and individual variation is real. Creatinine is heavily influenced by muscle mass, which matters on a GLP-1 because rapid weight loss can change muscle composition.

BUN (blood urea nitrogen). BUN measures a nitrogen-containing waste product from protein metabolism. Normal ranges are typically 7 to 20 mg/dL. BUN rises with dehydration, high-protein diets, and reduced kidney function. The ratio of BUN to creatinine can help distinguish dehydration from true kidney dysfunction: a high ratio often points to dehydration.

ACR (urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio). ACR measures how much of a protein called albumin is leaking into urine. Healthy kidneys keep albumin in blood. Leak means the kidney's filtering units may be damaged. An ACR under 30 mg/g is normal. 30 to 300 is "microalbuminuria," an early marker of kidney stress. Above 300 is significant proteinuria. ACR is the most sensitive early marker of diabetic kidney disease, which is why it matters so much on GLP-1 medications prescribed for type 2 diabetes.

The reading kidney labs post has a deeper walkthrough of each of these numbers and how they fit together.

What GLP-1s do to these numbers

GLP-1 medications can shift kidney labs in both directions, and context matters.

Creatinine can rise temporarily in the first weeks of treatment, often reflecting dehydration from GI side effects rather than true kidney damage. Reduced fluid volume concentrates creatinine in the blood. According to the FDA Ozempic prescribing information, cases of acute kidney injury have been reported, particularly during severe dehydration episodes.

Rapid weight loss on GLP-1s can also affect creatinine through changes in muscle mass. Creatinine is produced by muscle, so loss of muscle mass can lower creatinine independent of kidney function. This is a measurement artifact, not a real improvement in kidney function.

eGFR shifts with creatinine. If creatinine rises due to dehydration, eGFR drops temporarily. If creatinine falls due to muscle loss, eGFR can rise artifactually.

BUN commonly rises with dehydration on GLP-1s. A BUN-to-creatinine ratio above 20:1 often suggests dehydration rather than kidney dysfunction.

ACR is more interesting on GLP-1s. Multiple studies, including the FLOW trial published in the New England Journal of Medicine in 2024, have shown that semaglutide and similar medications can reduce albuminuria over time in people with type 2 diabetes and CKD. This is part of why GLP-1s are increasingly considered kidney-protective in diabetic populations.

The takeaway: short-term, GLP-1s can make some kidney labs look slightly worse, mostly through dehydration. Long-term, especially in people with type 2 diabetes, GLP-1s tend to improve kidney outcomes rather than harm them. The Ozempic kidney side effects post has the broader picture.

How to read your lab report

Your lab report usually shows your result, the reference range for that lab, and a flag (H, L, or *) if your number is outside the range.

A few principles:

Reference ranges are population averages, not individual guarantees. A value just outside the range isn't automatically concerning, and a value inside the range isn't automatically fine. Context matters.

Trends matter more than single values. One creatinine of 1.3 mg/dL in someone whose baseline was 1.0 is a different situation than someone whose baseline has always been 1.3. Compare to your own history.

Look at the BUN-to-creatinine ratio. If BUN is elevated more than creatinine (ratio above 20:1), dehydration is often the cause. If both are elevated proportionally, a more detailed workup may be warranted.

Look for ACR alongside the others. Elevated ACR is the earliest sign of diabetic kidney disease and can be present even when eGFR and creatinine are still normal.

Check whether the creatinine calculation used a "race-corrected" formula. Older eGFR equations included a race adjustment that has since been retired by most major medical societies. If your report shows two eGFR values (with and without race), use the one without.

If one value is slightly off and everything else looks good, the most common explanation is hydration state at the time of the draw. A repeat test after a few days of deliberate hydration often clarifies the picture.

Do not interpret your own results as a diagnosis. Reading your labs helps you have an informed conversation with your doctor. It doesn't replace that conversation.

Questions to bring to your doctor

A lab result worth discussing is worth discussing in person or on a call, not just via patient portal messages.

Useful questions:

What is my baseline on these labs from before I started the GLP-1? Having a pre-treatment number is the single most useful comparison.

Is this change consistent with dehydration, or does it point to something else? The BUN-to-creatinine ratio and ACR can help clarify.

Should we repeat the labs after I've been deliberately hydrating for a few days? This is often a reasonable next step for a mildly abnormal result.

Do I have any other risk factors for kidney disease (diabetes, high blood pressure, family history, NSAID use)? Risk factors affect how closely the result should be watched.

How often should I be having kidney labs drawn while on this medication? Quarterly is reasonable for many patients, more often for those with baseline kidney disease.

Does this result change how you want me to manage hydration, my dose, or anything else about the medication?

Home tracking between lab visits

You can't measure kidney function at home, but you can track the inputs that most affect the lab values.

Hydration is the biggest modifiable factor. Urine color in the pale-to-medium-yellow range consistently, not just on good days, reflects adequate hydration. Apps like Urivia let you log color patterns over time, which is especially useful when you're trying to sort out whether a recent creatinine bump was a one-off dehydration artifact or part of a longer trend.

Blood pressure is kidney-relevant. Home blood pressure cuffs are inexpensive and accurate enough for trend tracking. Keeping blood pressure in target range is a direct kidney-protective move.

Weight trends are worth watching. Rapid weight loss (more than two pounds per week) on a GLP-1 can include significant water loss, which shifts lab values. A slower, steadier loss is easier on the kidneys.

Foamy urine is a self-check for possible protein leak. Occasional foam is normal. Persistent heavy foam that doesn't clear after flushing is worth mentioning to your doctor, especially before your next lab draw.

Avoid NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, aspirin at pain-relieving doses) without specific medical guidance. NSAIDs can stress kidneys on their own, and the combination with GLP-1-induced dehydration amplifies the risk.

Frequently asked questions

What kidney labs should I get on Ozempic or Wegovy?

Standard kidney monitoring includes serum creatinine, eGFR, BUN, and urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (ACR). A basic metabolic panel covers the first three. ACR is a separate urine test that catches early protein leakage. Most endocrinologists and primary care doctors will order these routinely for patients on GLP-1s.

Can GLP-1 medications raise creatinine?

Yes, temporarily, usually because of dehydration from GI side effects. A creatinine rise in the first weeks of a new dose often resolves with better hydration. Persistent elevation despite adequate fluid intake warrants further evaluation.

Are GLP-1s bad for kidneys?

Overall, no, especially in people with type 2 diabetes. Large trials have shown reduced risk of major kidney disease progression with semaglutide. The risk is specifically acute kidney injury during severe dehydration episodes, which is avoidable with adequate fluids. The Ozempic kidney side effects post covers the evidence in detail.

How often should I have my kidneys checked on a GLP-1?

For most patients without baseline kidney disease, every three to six months is reasonable. For patients with CKD or diabetes-related kidney changes, more frequent monitoring (often quarterly) is typical. Your doctor calibrates based on your history and current labs.

What does a low eGFR mean on Ozempic?

A low eGFR can reflect acute dehydration (often reversible), muscle mass changes from weight loss (a measurement artifact), or true kidney function decline. Your doctor will consider which is most likely based on your other labs, symptoms, and trend. A single low value rarely tells the full story.

Should I stop my GLP-1 if my labs are off?

Never stop a prescription medication without your doctor's input. Mildly abnormal labs rarely justify stopping a medication that's benefiting you. Your doctor may adjust timing, dose, or recheck the lab after hydration correction. DIY discontinuation can cause its own problems.
