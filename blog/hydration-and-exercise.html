Hydration and Exercise: Before, During, After

By the UriVia Health team Last updated April 2026

Most healthy adults should drink 16-20 ounces of water 2-3 hours before exercise, 6-12 ounces every 15-20 minutes during activity, and 16-24 ounces for every pound lost during the workout afterward. Electrolytes become relevant for sessions longer than 60 minutes or in hot conditions. Your sweat rate is highly individual, and the most accurate hydration plan comes from weighing yourself before and after training.

The pre-workout window

The goal of pre-workout hydration is to start exercise with your body in a well-hydrated state without heading in with so much fluid that you cramp or have to stop to urinate mid-session. The timing matters as much as the volume.

The American College of Sports Medicine recommends drinking roughly 16-20 ounces (500-600ml) of water or sports drink 2-3 hours before exercise, then another 8-12 ounces (200-300ml) about 10-20 minutes before starting. This timing allows excess fluid to be excreted before your workout begins while ensuring you start with optimal hydration.

Pre-workout urine color is a useful check. Pale straw yellow indicates you're ready. Dark yellow or amber means you should drink more before starting. If you're already dehydrated at the start of a workout, no amount of mid-session drinking will fully compensate, especially for high-intensity or endurance sessions.

Morning workouts create a specific challenge because you're starting from an overnight fluid deficit. A morning exerciser who skips pre-workout drinking is often noticeably dehydrated by the second set. 8-16 ounces of water within 15-30 minutes of waking, plus additional pre-workout intake, helps most morning trainers significantly.

For fasted training (common with certain fitness approaches), electrolyte intake matters more. Without food providing sodium, potassium, and magnesium, plain water can deplete your baseline electrolyte levels, contributing to early fatigue and poor performance.

During exercise

For sessions under 60 minutes at moderate intensity, plain water at 6-12 ounces every 15-20 minutes is sufficient for nearly everyone. You don't need sports drinks or electrolyte mixes for a regular gym workout, spin class, or short run. Your body has enough glycogen and electrolyte stores to handle sessions this length without supplementation.

For sessions over 60 minutes, especially in heat or at high intensity, the equation changes. The ACSM guidance suggests adding carbohydrates (30-60g per hour) and electrolytes (primarily sodium at 200-300mg per hour) for prolonged sessions. This is where actual sports drinks earn their keep: Gatorade, Powerade, Tailwind, and similar products are designed for exactly this scenario.

Sweat rates vary enormously between individuals, and you can only find your own through measurement. Elite endurance athletes can lose 1.5-2 liters per hour in hot conditions. Recreational exercisers typically lose 0.5-1 liter per hour. A 185-pound cyclist in August and a 130-pound yoga practitioner in February have nothing in common in terms of fluid needs, and generic advice cannot account for that gap.

Thirst during exercise is less reliable than thirst at rest. Many athletes don't feel strongly thirsty until they're 2-3 percent dehydrated, which is already affecting performance measurably. Drinking to a schedule during long sessions produces better outcomes than drinking only when thirsty.

Over-drinking during exercise is also possible and genuinely dangerous. Exercise-associated hyponatremia (dangerously low blood sodium from too much water during prolonged activity) is a real risk in endurance events. The rule: match intake to sweat losses, don't exceed them. If you gain weight during a long event, you're drinking too much.

Post-workout recovery

Post-exercise rehydration is straightforward: replace about 125-150 percent of the fluid lost during the session. Weigh yourself before and after a workout (same clothing, or nude, to reduce variability). Each pound of weight lost represents approximately 16 ounces of fluid. Multiply by 1.25-1.5 to get your rehydration target.

For a typical gym session where you lose 1 pound, that's 20-24 ounces of fluid afterward, spread over 2-3 hours. For a 2-hour endurance workout with 3-4 pounds of loss, that's 60-80 ounces of fluid over 4-6 hours, and electrolyte replacement becomes important to absorb it effectively.

The reason to aim for 125-150 percent rather than exactly 100 percent is that your body continues to lose fluid through sweat and urine during the recovery period. Drinking only the amount you lost will leave you still slightly dehydrated by evening.

Post-workout urine color is a reasonable check. If it's pale straw yellow within 2-3 hours of finishing, you've rehydrated well. If it's still dark yellow or amber hours after training, you need more fluid, and potentially electrolytes if you've been drinking plain water and feel bloated rather than rehydrated.

Protein and carbohydrate recovery meals help rehydration because they contain water and electrolytes naturally. A post-workout meal with protein, carbs, salt, and some fluid is more effective for total recovery than water alone. This is one of the reasons athletes generally don't need expensive recovery drinks if they're eating real food.

Electrolytes in the mix

For workouts under 60 minutes in moderate conditions, plain water is fine and electrolyte drinks are unnecessary. For longer sessions, hot conditions, or high-sweat athletes, electrolytes become important.

Sodium is the primary electrolyte lost through sweat (typically 300-800mg per liter of sweat, varying widely between individuals) and the primary one that needs replacement. Potassium, magnesium, and calcium are also lost but in much smaller quantities, and adequate dietary intake usually covers these.

Sweat sodium losses are genetically variable. Some people are "salty sweaters" who lose 1000+ mg per liter and leave visible salt streaks on clothing and skin. Others lose 200-300mg per liter and rarely need electrolyte replacement for normal training. The only way to know where you fall is to notice how you respond to different hydration strategies over time.

Commercial sports drinks typically contain 100-200mg of sodium and 20-30g of sugar per 12-ounce serving. The sodium is useful during extended training; the sugar may or may not be, depending on whether you're fueling a workout or just sipping between gym sets. For training without the need for carbohydrate fuel, lower-sugar options like Liquid I.V., LMNT, or plain salted water work well. electrolytes vs water covers this in more detail.

Homemade options work too. A simple sports drink: 16 ounces water, 1/4 teaspoon salt, 2-3 tablespoons of real fruit juice, and a small amount of honey or sugar for longer sessions. This costs pennies per serving and performs similarly to commercial products for most training scenarios.

Tracking sweat loss individually

The single most useful measurement in hydration for exercise is your individual sweat rate. Here's how to measure it:

Weigh yourself nude (or in known-weight clothing) immediately before a typical workout. Note the weight. Complete your normal session without drinking anything if possible, or carefully track exactly what you drink. Weigh yourself again immediately after, in the same clothing state. Calculate: (pre-weight − post-weight) + any fluid consumed = total sweat loss.

Do this across different conditions: a summer outdoor session versus a winter indoor session. A high-intensity workout versus a moderate one. Fasted versus fed. You'll quickly see that your sweat rate isn't a single number but varies based on conditions, and your hydration plan should vary with it.

Typical ranges once you measure: moderate indoor training produces 0.4-0.8 liters per hour, hot outdoor training can exceed 1.5 liters per hour, and high-intensity or endurance sessions in heat can hit 2+ liters per hour for larger athletes.

When to see a doctor

Cramping that doesn't resolve with normal rehydration and electrolyte intake, dizziness on standing after workouts that doesn't improve within an hour, or persistent fatigue that lasts days after exercise can indicate more significant issues. Severe exertional cramps, muscle damage (dark brown urine after intense exercise), or heat stroke symptoms (confusion, ceased sweating, core body temperature above 104°F) are emergencies.

Exercise-associated hyponatremia presents with headache, nausea, confusion, and swelling during or after prolonged endurance events. It's caused by over-consuming water relative to sodium loss and is a medical emergency requiring hospital-level treatment.

People with heart disease, kidney disease, or on fluid-affecting medications should follow their doctor's specific hydration guidance for exercise rather than general athletic formulas. What works for a healthy recreational runner may be inappropriate for someone managing a cardiac or renal condition.

If you consistently feel worse after workouts (more tired, more drained, slower to recover), and improving hydration doesn't change that pattern, an evaluation for underlying conditions (anemia, thyroid issues, electrolyte imbalances, undiagnosed diabetes) is reasonable.

How to track this yourself

Simple tracking beats complicated tracking for most people. Track your weight before and after workouts occasionally (monthly is fine). Track urine color before, mid-workout if possible, and an hour after. Notice how you feel during and after training.

Apps like Urivia let you track urine color patterns over time, which can show whether your training sessions are consistently leaving you in hydration deficit or whether you're recovering well. If your post-workout urine is consistently amber or darker, your current strategy isn't working for the conditions you're training in.

Over weeks, patterns emerge. You'll notice that certain workouts, certain seasons, or certain situations (travel, early morning training, big meals before training) affect how you hydrate. Adjust accordingly rather than following generic formulas that don't account for your specific reality.

Frequently asked questions

Is it okay to drink nothing during a short workout?

For sessions under 45-60 minutes at moderate intensity, yes, especially if you're well-hydrated going in. You won't lose enough fluid to matter. For longer or more intense sessions, skipping all fluids hurts performance and recovery.

Do I need to drink during weight lifting?

Not always, but it helps. Moderate sips between sets keep you hydrated without filling your stomach. A 90-minute lifting session with no water is fine for most people but suboptimal in warm gyms or during high-volume training.

Should I drink more if I sweat a lot?

Yes, and probably more than you think. Heavy sweaters lose more fluid and more sodium, and they often need electrolyte replacement even for shorter sessions. If you find salt crusted on your face or clothes after workouts, you're losing significant sodium and plain water alone may not be enough.

How do I know if I'm over-hydrating during exercise?

Signs include bloating during training, gaining weight during workouts (weighing more after than before), gurgling stomach, and in extreme cases nausea or headache from low blood sodium. If this happens, cut back on fluid intake, add electrolytes, and stop forcing water when you're not thirsty.

What about hot yoga?

Hot yoga can produce significant sweat losses despite being relatively low-intensity. Most hot yoga studios encourage hydration before class and some water during. A 90-minute hot yoga session often benefits from electrolyte drinks afterward, especially for frequent practitioners.

Can I rehydrate too fast after a workout?

Chugging large volumes (20+ ounces in a few minutes) produces brief overhydration and rapid urination without fully restoring fluid stores. Steady drinking over 1-2 hours is more effective.

---

Good exercise hydration is about matching intake to output across the full cycle: well-hydrated going in, steady during, deliberate recovery after. The specifics are individual, and measurement beats generic rules.
