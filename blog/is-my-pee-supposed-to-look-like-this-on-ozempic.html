Is My Pee Supposed to Look Like This on Ozempic?

By the UriVia Health team Last updated April 2026

Yes, urine color changes are extremely common on Ozempic, and most of them are not dangerous. If you're reading this late at night because your pee looked off and you got worried, take a breath. Darker urine almost always means dehydration, which is the most common side effect chain on GLP-1 medications. There are a few specific colors that warrant a call to your doctor, but the majority of changes on Ozempic are things you can address with water.

This post walks through what you're probably seeing, what it means, and when it's worth more than a glass of water.

The honest answer (yes, changes are common)

If your urine has shifted since starting Ozempic, you're not making it up, and you're not the only one. The Mayo Clinic notes that hydration status is the single biggest driver of urine color in healthy adults. Ozempic reliably reduces hydration in the first weeks of treatment because it suppresses appetite, blunts thirst, and often causes GI side effects that reduce fluid intake and sometimes increase fluid loss.

According to the FDA prescribing information for Ozempic, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, decreased appetite, and constipation are among the most common side effects. All of these affect hydration. The darker urine you're seeing is almost always the visible consequence of that chain, not a sign that the medication is damaging you.

The second most common cause of urine color change on Ozempic is simply eating less. Fruits, vegetables, soups, and yogurt contribute significantly to daily fluid intake. When you eat less, you drink less through food even before you think about it.

None of this is a reason to panic at 2 a.m. It is a reason to drink more water tomorrow and to watch the pattern over the next few days. The Ozempic urine color post has the broader picture of what's happening physiologically.

The 5 color ranges and what they mean

Clear or nearly clear. You're drinking a lot, possibly more than you need. This is usually fine, but if your urine stays clear for days on end, you may be diluting your electrolytes and could benefit from some salt in your diet.

Pale straw to medium yellow. This is the healthy range. Your hydration is good. Nothing to worry about.

Dark yellow. The edge of dehydration. Your body is asking for more water. This is by far the most common reading for new Ozempic users, especially first thing in the morning or after a day with poor intake.

Amber or honey-colored. Clear dehydration. Your fluid deficit is real. Drink steadily over the rest of the day and check your color in the morning.

Brown, tea-colored, pink, red, or cola-colored. These are not hydration issues. Brown can indicate muscle or liver issues in rare cases. Pink, red, or cola-colored urine can indicate blood. Cola-colored in particular should trigger a same-day call to your doctor. Pink or red could be from beets, food dye, or certain medications, but blood is possible and worth ruling out.

When it's just dehydration

Most likely, what you're seeing is just dehydration. The signs that fit this pattern are:

Your urine is dark yellow to amber, not brown or reddish. The color shifts lighter when you drink deliberately over a day. You haven't had symptoms beyond general tiredness, dry mouth, or the GI side effects you expect from Ozempic. You don't have fever, severe pain, or blood in the urine.

If that describes what you're seeing, the path forward is straightforward. Increase water intake steadily over the next 24 hours, include some electrolytes if you've been vomiting or having diarrhea, and recheck your color tomorrow morning and again in the afternoon. Most dehydration-driven color shifts resolve in one to two days of consistent intake. The Ozempic dehydration post has the full practical guide to fluid intake on Ozempic.

Morning urine is naturally darker than afternoon urine. This is not a GLP-1 issue, it's just physiology. You go hours overnight without drinking, and your kidneys concentrate urine to conserve water. What you're watching for is whether the afternoon comes back into the healthy yellow range. If it does, you're fine.

When it's something more

A few scenarios move this out of the "just drink water" category and into the "call your doctor" category.

Dark urine that doesn't lighten after two to three days of deliberate hydration. At that point, water alone is not the issue, and something else deserves attention.

Brown or tea-colored urine. This can be dehydration, but it can also indicate muscle breakdown (rhabdomyolysis) or liver-related issues. If brown urine doesn't clear in a day of real hydration, call your doctor.

Pink, red, or cola-colored urine. These can indicate blood in the urine. Food coloring from beets or certain medications can also cause these colors, but blood is worth ruling out rather than assuming.

Dark urine plus symptoms. Fever, severe back or side pain, persistent vomiting, swelling in your feet or face, confusion, fainting, or a noticeable drop in how much you're urinating all shift this into urgent territory. Don't wait on these.

Persistent heavy foam or bubbles in the urine that don't clear after flushing. This can indicate protein in the urine, which is worth a doctor visit even without other symptoms.

Tracking over time

Once-off readings are less useful than patterns. Ozempic effects shift with dose, time of day, what you ate yesterday, and where you are in the injection cycle. A single dark morning is not a crisis. Four dark mornings in a row, or a week where your afternoon readings stay amber, is a pattern worth paying attention to.

A simple habit is to check your color in the morning and again in the afternoon, and pay attention to where the readings sit across the full week. Apps like Urivia let you log color patterns over time, which makes it easier to see whether your hydration is actually trending in the right direction as you adjust your intake.

If you'd rather not use an app, a note in your phone or a paper journal works. The tool matters less than the habit. Once you have two to three weeks of readings, you'll have a personal baseline that tells you what's normal for your body on Ozempic. Deviations from that baseline are easier to notice than shifts from a generic "should be medium yellow" reference.

Frequently asked questions

Is dark urine dangerous on Ozempic?

Most of the time, no. Dark urine is the most common hydration signal on GLP-1 medications, and it usually resolves with consistent water intake. It becomes worth medical attention if it persists for more than two to three days of deliberate hydration, or if it appears alongside other symptoms like fever, pain, swelling, or a drop in urination.

Should I stop taking Ozempic if my urine is dark?

Never stop a medication without talking to your doctor. Dark urine alone is rarely a reason to stop Ozempic. It's much more likely a reason to drink more water. If dark urine persists despite hydration, call your doctor for guidance on what to do next.

Is it normal for urine to be darker in the morning on Ozempic?

Yes. Morning urine is naturally more concentrated because you go hours overnight without drinking. A darker morning reading is normal on or off Ozempic. What matters is whether your afternoon reading returns to the healthy yellow range.

Can Ozempic cause blood in the urine?

Ozempic is not known to directly cause blood in the urine. Blood in the urine has many possible causes including infection, kidney stones, or other issues. Pink, red, or cola-colored urine warrants a call to your doctor regardless of whether you're on Ozempic.

How long do urine changes last on Ozempic?

For most users, the most pronounced changes happen in the first four to six weeks as the body adjusts to the medication and GI side effects are most intense. Color typically stabilizes as side effects ease, though dose increases can briefly reset this cycle.

Should I drink more water if my pee looks dark on Ozempic?

Yes, almost always. Spread intake across the day in smaller volumes rather than trying to chug a large amount at once. If water alone doesn't shift your color within 48 hours of real effort, that's the point where a doctor call makes sense.
