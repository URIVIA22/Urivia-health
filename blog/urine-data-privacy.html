Privacy and Urine Data: How Urivia Handles Your Health Information

By the UriVia Health team Last updated April 2026

Urivia is built local-first. Your scan photos never leave your device. Your scan history is stored on your phone, not on our servers. You don't need an account, email address, or any personal information to use the app. The Pro weekly insights feature sends anonymized scan data to an AI provider for summarization, and you can opt out. If you delete the app, your data is gone. That's the complete picture.

What Urivia stores

On your phone, Urivia stores three things: your scan history (color readings, timestamps, confidence scores), any symptoms or context you logged with each scan, and your settings (reminders, medication type if you set one, unit preferences). That's it.

There's no profile of you. There's no health record being built in the background. There's no database on our servers that has your name, email, or medical conditions attached to your scans. If you handed us your phone right now and asked "what does Urivia know about me that isn't visible in the app?" the honest answer would be: nothing. Everything the app knows is what you can see yourself.

The scan data is stored using AsyncStorage, which is a standard on-device storage system in the React Native framework the app is built on. It's the same category of storage that handles your settings in most mobile apps. The data is associated with your device, not with an account, and not synced anywhere unless you explicitly enable sync features we haven't built yet.

Photos of your urine do not get saved in the app's history. The scan process reads color values from the photo in memory, computes a match against the color library, saves that match to your history, and the photo itself is discarded. If you ever want to verify this, the dev panel (hidden behind tapping the version footer five times) shows the raw scan data that's stored; photos aren't in there because they're not kept.

What Urivia never shares

We don't sell your data. We don't share it with advertisers. We don't provide it to insurers. We don't upload it to cloud storage by default. Your scan history is yours and stays on your phone.

The Pro weekly insights feature is the one exception, and it's opt-in. When you use the weekly insight, the app sends your scan data from the past seven days to a large language model (Claude Haiku, through a secure proxy) to generate a natural-language summary. What gets sent: the color readings, timestamps, symptoms logged, and general context like which medication you set in onboarding. What doesn't get sent: photos, any identifying information about you, your name, email, device identifiers, or anything that could tie the data back to you specifically.

The model returns a summary, the summary gets shown in the app, and the request ends. Nothing about the request is stored by us or by the AI provider in a way that's tied to your identity. The AI provider's standard retention policies apply (brief retention for safety review), and those policies are available to read in their documentation. We chose this provider specifically because of their privacy posture.

If you don't want any scan data leaving your device ever, the weekly insights feature can be turned off in settings, and the rest of the app will continue to work exactly the same. Color scanning, history, symptom logging, and reminders all run entirely on-device.

Local-first architecture explained

"Local-first" is a specific architectural choice that's become rarer as cloud-based apps have become the default. It means the app treats your device as the primary location for your data, not a copy of data that lives elsewhere.

Most health apps work the other way around. You create an account, your data lives on the company's servers, and the app shows you a view of your own data through that server connection. If the company shuts down, the servers go offline, or they change their privacy policy, your data is exposed to all of that.

Local-first inverts this. Your data lives on your device from the moment it's created. The app doesn't need a server connection to function. The company running the app doesn't have a copy of your data unless you explicitly opt into features that require one.

The practical implications: Urivia works offline. Urivia doesn't have a way to see your data on their end. Urivia can't hand your data to a subpoena or a data breach attacker because Urivia doesn't have your data. If Urivia shut down tomorrow, your scan history would remain on your phone until you deleted the app.

The trade-offs: if you lose your phone, you lose your scan history (unless you've enabled phone backups through iCloud or Google, which store the app data with your other app data). Switching phones requires a migration path (which we're building). Multi-device sync requires either cloud storage (which would change the privacy model) or direct device-to-device transfer (which we're exploring). For users who care about privacy more than convenience, local-first is a good trade. For users who want seamless multi-device sync, it's a compromise.

We chose local-first because we think health data is different from your music library or your to-do list. You should not have to hand your bodily function history to a company you barely know in order to track it.

What you can delete and how

You have complete control over your data in Urivia.

Deleting an individual scan: open that scan in the history view, tap delete. It's gone from the app immediately.

Deleting all history: settings, "delete all scan data," confirm. All scan records are removed.

Deleting the app entirely: standard iOS or Android app uninstall. All local data goes with it, because all your data is local.

If you used the Pro weekly insights feature, the summary text that was generated and shown to you is stored in your scan history if you saved it, and gets deleted along with everything else. The original AI provider's records of the request follow their standard retention policy, which is brief and does not connect to your identity.

There's no "download your data" feature because there's nothing for us to give you. If you want your data, it's already on your phone. The export feature in the app lets you generate a PDF report of your scan history for medical appointments, which you can save, email, or share as you choose.

Our privacy commitments

We make the following commitments, and we plan to keep making them:

Your scan photos will never leave your device. We built it this way deliberately and we will not change this without making the change extremely explicit to users.

Your scan data will never be sold to third parties. Not to advertisers, not to insurers, not to data brokers, not to researchers without your explicit consent for a specific study.

We will not introduce tracking or analytics that identify you personally. The app uses PostHog for basic product analytics (app opens, feature usage, crash reports), but this is configured to not collect personally identifying information, and there's a privacy toggle in settings that turns it off entirely. You can use Urivia with zero analytics being collected if you want.

We will not add features that change the privacy model without notifying users clearly and giving them the ability to opt out. If we ever add cloud sync (as some users have requested), it will be opt-in with clear explanation of what changes. Default will always remain local-first.

We will not share data with law enforcement or government agencies without a valid legal process, and because we don't have most of your data, the scope of what could even be shared is extremely limited by design.

When privacy matters clinically

Health data privacy matters for reasons beyond general preference. Data breaches happen regularly in health tech, and apps holding millions of users' data on centralized servers are high-value targets. By holding essentially no centralized user data, we remove this entire category of risk.

Ownership of your own health data is also a principle worth defending. You generated it, observing your own body. It should belong to you by default, not to the app that helped you observe it.

How to track this without giving up privacy

The core promise of a privacy-respecting tracking app is that you can get the tracking benefit without paying a privacy cost. Urivia is designed around this.

You can use the app indefinitely without creating an account. You can use it without internet. You can delete everything at any time. You can export your own data in formats you control. The app's role is to help you observe your body; it is not to build a relationship of data dependency.

Apps like Urivia let you track color patterns over time while keeping that data under your control. Not every health app makes this choice. The ones that do are worth paying attention to, especially for data as sensitive as anything involving your body's functions.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need an email or phone number to use Urivia?

No. The app works without any identifying information. You can install and use every feature without creating an account or providing personal details.

Is my scan history backed up if I use iCloud or Google Drive?

If your phone is configured to back up app data through iCloud (iOS) or Google One (Android), your Urivia data will be included in those backups like any other app's local data. This backup goes to your Apple or Google account, not to us. Disable app data backup for Urivia in your phone's settings if you don't want this.

Does Urivia have access to my photos?

Urivia requests camera access to take scan photos. It does not request access to your existing photo library. The camera is used in the moment of scanning, and the photo is analyzed and discarded. Urivia never accesses photos you've taken outside the app.

What information goes to the AI for weekly insights?

Scan color readings, timestamps, symptoms you logged, and contextual settings (medication type if you set one). No photos, no identifiers, no information that could tie the data back to you. The feature is opt-in and can be disabled in settings.

What happens if Urivia shuts down?

Your data is on your phone and remains there until you delete the app. The core app would continue to function for scanning and local tracking. Cloud-dependent features (weekly insights) would stop working. You'd have your complete history intact.

Can I see what Urivia has collected about me?

Everything the app knows about you is visible in the app itself: your scan history, your settings, your logged symptoms. There's no hidden profile or behind-the-scenes data. The dev panel (tap the version footer 5 times) shows the raw storage if you want to verify.

---

Privacy is an architectural choice, not a marketing claim. We built Urivia local-first because we think that's the right default for health data. If the privacy posture matters to you, Urivia is one of the few apps in this space that's structured around protecting it.
