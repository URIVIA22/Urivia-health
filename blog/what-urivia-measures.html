What Urivia Measures (and What It Doesn't)

By the UriVia Health team Last updated April 2026

Urivia tracks urine color over time and helps you notice patterns in your hydration. It observes trends, correlates color changes with symptoms you log, and gives you a running history you can share with your doctor. It does not diagnose, detect, or screen for any medical condition. It does not replace a urinalysis, a blood test, or a doctor's visit. Here's the complete picture of what the app does, what it doesn't, and why we draw the line where we do.

What Urivia tracks

The core thing Urivia tracks is urine color. Every scan creates a timestamped record of what color the app read, your confidence score, any symptoms you logged, and the signal category the app assigned (normal, monitor, seek care, or urgent).

Over time, these records become a hydration history. You can look back over days, weeks, or months and see how consistently you fall in the healthy range versus how often you drift into mild or moderate dehydration. For people whose urine color varies with specific triggers (certain meals, medications, travel, menstrual cycle, exercise patterns), those correlations become visible after a few weeks of tracking.

Alongside color, the app tracks optional context. If you log symptoms before analyzing a scan (back pain, burning, fever, blood in urine, reduced urination frequency), that context gets factored into the signal the app gives you. It also gets saved with the scan, so if you ever want to show a doctor, "this is what I've been seeing, and these are the symptoms I had at the time," you have that record.

For people on GLP-1 medications, the app tracks additional context specific to that class: the medication itself, how many weeks in, any relevant side effects. This feeds into the signal logic because GLP-1 users have different hydration patterns than the general population, and those patterns are worth distinguishing.

That's it for measurement. Urivia tracks what you can see (urine color) and what you can describe (symptoms and context). It does not measure what you can't see.

What Urivia patterns

The app looks at trends across your scan history and surfaces patterns that are hard to notice day-by-day.

Simple patterns include things like: most of your amber readings happen on weekday afternoons, or your hydration drops every time you travel, or your morning urine is darker on days when you didn't drink water in the evening. These are observational findings. The app isn't inferring causation; it's showing you the data and letting you notice what matches.

For Pro users, the weekly personalized insight feature uses an AI model to summarize patterns in natural language. Instead of scrolling through 30 scan records, you get a paragraph saying, "Your hydration was consistent Monday through Wednesday, dipped Thursday and Friday into the monitor zone, and recovered over the weekend." You could derive the same information from the raw data; the summary just makes it faster.

Patterns matter more than individual scans. A single dark yellow reading tells you almost nothing. Forty readings over six weeks tell you quite a bit: what your normal range is, how often you leave it, what triggers correlate with drift. Apps are good at this kind of aggregation in a way that mental tracking isn't.

What Urivia never claims

Urivia does not detect, diagnose, screen for, or predict any medical condition. This isn't marketing language; it's a hard line in what the app is designed to do, what it's allowed to do under FDA wellness app guidance, and what we think is honest given what color analysis can and can't tell you.

The app does not detect kidney disease. Urine color can shift for many reasons that have nothing to do with kidneys, and kidney disease in its early stages often produces no visible urine changes at all. Only a blood test (eGFR) or a specific urine test (albumin-to-creatinine ratio) can assess kidney function, and those require lab equipment the app doesn't have.

The app does not detect urinary tract infections. UTIs require identifying bacteria in the urine, either through a urinalysis with leukocyte esterase and nitrite testing, or through a urine culture. An app looking at color cannot determine whether bacteria are present. Cloudy urine may suggest it, but confirmation requires testing strips or a lab.

The app does not detect blood at microscopic levels. Visible blood (urine that's clearly pink or red) is obvious enough that the app will flag it. But microscopic hematuria, which can be present without visible color change, requires a urine dipstick or lab analysis. This matters because microscopic hematuria can be an early sign of kidney or bladder issues worth evaluating.

The app does not detect glucose, ketones, protein, bilirubin, or other chemical markers. Those require dipstick strips or lab testing.

The app does not monitor for diabetes, hypertension, or any specific condition. People managing these conditions may find Urivia useful for hydration patterns, but it is not a condition-management tool.

If you see marketing language anywhere claiming Urivia can do any of the above, flag it and we'll correct it. The line is deliberate and we hold it.

Why we're careful with language

The reason we're careful about what the app claims comes down to two things: what's actually provable, and what's legally permitted.

What's provable: color analysis from a phone camera can reliably distinguish the major hydration spectrum shades under good conditions. It cannot reliably distinguish clinical conditions. Saying "Urivia helps you track color patterns" is accurate. Saying "Urivia detects early kidney disease" would be inaccurate and misleading, regardless of whether it would help the app sell better.

What's legally permitted: FDA guidance distinguishes between general wellness apps (which can track, notice, and pattern) and medical devices (which can detect, diagnose, and screen for disease). Medical devices require a clinical validation pathway, regulatory approval, and an entirely different compliance infrastructure. Urivia is built as a wellness app, and the language in the app, on the website, and in marketing matches that. The verbs we use ("track, pattern, notice, monitor") are allowed. The verbs we avoid ("detect, diagnose, screen, prevent") would move the app into medical device territory.

This isn't just a legal compliance exercise. It's also an honesty boundary. If Urivia claimed to detect disease and someone relied on that claim instead of seeing a doctor, we could contribute to a genuinely bad outcome. Keeping the language tight keeps the app's role clear: it's a tool for awareness, not for diagnosis.

How to use Urivia alongside your doctor

The most useful way to use Urivia is as a source of information you bring to medical appointments, not as a replacement for them.

Before an appointment, you can review your recent scan history and flag any patterns worth discussing. "My hydration has been consistently in the monitor zone for three weeks even though I'm drinking what feels like enough water" is a useful thing to tell a doctor. "I noticed my urine was dark orange for two days last month" is a useful thing to tell a doctor. Pulling these out of the app is easier than trying to remember.

During an appointment, you can share specific scan records if the doctor asks. The app's export function produces a summary you can email or show on screen. Some users have reported that their doctors found the tracking data more useful than verbal recall, especially for chronic conditions where trends matter.

After an appointment, if your doctor gives you hydration goals or asks you to track symptoms, the app can help you execute that. Logging symptoms and tracking color patterns over weeks gives you data to bring back to a follow-up.

Apps like Urivia work best when they're one input among many, not the whole picture. Your doctor has access to lab tests, imaging, physical exams, and medical history. The app adds daily observational data that fills in the gaps between visits. That combination is more useful than either one alone.

When to see a doctor

Regardless of what Urivia shows, see a doctor if you have: persistent red, pink, or brown urine; urine color changes accompanied by fever, back pain, burning, or reduced urination; known diabetes, CKD, or hypertension (regular monitoring is essential); persistent dehydration signs that don't improve with normal fluid intake; or any symptom pattern that feels concerning.

The app may flag readings as "seek care" or "urgent" based on color combined with symptoms. Take those seriously. The thresholds are designed to err toward caution.

If you see a reading you don't understand, the safest response is almost always to drink some water, retake under good lighting, and see what changes. If nothing changes over 24 hours, or if you have symptoms, get medical evaluation.

Tracking consistently

The app is more useful if you use it consistently. A scan a day, roughly at the same time, builds a baseline that's much more informative than sporadic scanning when something feels off. Most users settle into scanning either first thing in the morning (for a baseline) or mid-afternoon (to catch the most variable part of the hydration cycle).

Apps like Urivia let you track these patterns over time, which works best when you actually track. Forgetting to scan for three weeks and then scanning once because something looks different won't give you the context you need to interpret the reading.

Frequently asked questions

Can Urivia tell if I have a UTI?

No. Urivia observes color, not chemistry. A UTI requires identifying bacteria, typically through a urine dipstick with leukocyte esterase and nitrite, or through a urine culture. If you have UTI symptoms (burning, frequency, urgency, cloudy urine), see a doctor for testing.

Can Urivia detect kidney disease?

No. Kidney disease is assessed through blood tests (creatinine, eGFR) and specific urine tests (albumin-to-creatinine ratio), not through color alone. Urivia can help you notice if your hydration patterns are changing, which is information you can bring to a nephrologist, but it does not detect disease.

Does Urivia replace urinalysis strips?

No. Urinalysis strips measure chemical markers (glucose, ketones, protein, blood, pH, specific gravity) that Urivia cannot see. If your doctor has asked you to use strips, keep using them. Urivia complements strip-based testing by adding color pattern tracking over time.

What about the weekly AI insights?

The insights summarize patterns in your existing scan data. They don't generate new medical interpretations. If the insight mentions a trend, the underlying data is visible to you in the app. The AI is summarizing, not diagnosing.

Can Urivia replace a doctor's visit?

Absolutely not, and it's not designed to. The app helps you notice patterns. A doctor is equipped to interpret those patterns medically, order tests, diagnose conditions, and prescribe treatment. Two different jobs.

Why doesn't Urivia do dipstick analysis too?

We've considered it, but adding dipstick chemistry substantially changes what the app is. Dipsticks require physical strips, specific lighting, and analysis that approaches medical device territory. We may build this in the future; for now, the app focuses on doing color tracking well rather than doing more things less well.
