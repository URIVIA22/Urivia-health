Why I Built Urivia

By the UriVia Health team Last updated April 2026

I built Urivia because I noticed something strange in my own urine one morning, and the hour I spent trying to figure out what it meant was infuriating. There was no good way to get a calm, informed answer on my phone. So I built one. This is the story of why, and what we've committed to never do.

I'm writing this as the founder, not as a marketer. If you're here trying to decide whether to trust this app with anything personal, I want you to understand who's behind it and what the rules are.

The problem I kept seeing

It started with bubbles. One morning I noticed a lot of foam in my urine that didn't flush away. I didn't know what it meant. Was it normal, the kind of foam anyone would see on a strong stream? Was it protein, which can be a kidney signal? Was it something I should worry about or something I'd forget about by lunch?

So I did what most people do. I pulled out my phone and started Googling. Then I started image-searching, scrolling through forum photos and stock images, squinting at my bathroom and squinting at my screen, trying to decide if any of the pictures online actually matched what I was looking at. Twenty minutes later I wasn't closer to an answer. I was just more anxious.

That was the moment the app clicked in my head. I thought, it would be so much easier if I could just take a picture of what I'm actually seeing, and get feedback based on that photo, not on whether a random forum image kind of resembles it.

What struck me afterward was that the information I needed wasn't hidden. The Mayo Clinic has a clear page explaining what different urine colors can and cannot indicate. The National Kidney Foundation publishes guidance on when foamy urine is meaningful versus normal. The problem wasn't the information. The problem was the interface.

A one-time Google search is a terrible tool for a signal that only matters as a pattern. One odd morning means almost nothing. Fourteen odd mornings in a row, or a slow drift over a month, is a conversation with your doctor. The gap between those two things is where people get lost.

Why existing tools fall short

There are three tools most people reach for, and each one has a real limit.

The first is a Google search. It's free and instant, but it can't see you. It doesn't know you started a new medication three weeks ago, or that you've been traveling, or that today is the fourth day your color has looked off. It gives you the same generic answer it gives everyone else. And if you try to image-search to match what you're seeing, you're comparing your real bathroom under real lighting to a stock photo taken under studio conditions. That comparison isn't fair, and it isn't useful.

The second is a urine test strip. Strips are genuinely useful for specific things like protein, glucose, and pH. But strips require ordering supplies, reading tiny color squares under bad bathroom lighting, and logging the result somewhere. Most people try them twice and quit. The post on what Urivia actually measures explains why we chose color as our starting point rather than strips.

The third is a full clinical workup. A proper urinalysis at your doctor's office is the gold standard, and nothing we build will ever replace it. But most people get one once a year, if that. A year is a long time to miss a pattern.

What was missing was a middle layer. Something free or close to it, quick enough to actually do every day, private enough to trust, and smart enough to show you your own trend instead of a generic chart.

What we built

Urivia is a phone app that lets you photograph a urine sample against a calibration background, analyzes the color, and logs it so you can see your pattern over time. That's the whole product in one sentence.

The technical side is more interesting than it sounds. Phone cameras vary wildly. Bathroom lighting is some of the worst lighting in the world for color analysis. A naive "take a photo, read the color" app would be useless within a week. Most of our build time went into the unglamorous parts: calibration, multi-sample averaging, handling different lighting conditions, and a confidence score that tells you when a reading isn't trustworthy. The post on how Urivia's AI reads urine color walks through how the pipeline actually works if you want the technical version.

On top of the color analysis, there's a light layer of personalization. If you tell Urivia you're on a GLP-1 medication, managing diabetes, or being cautious about kidney health, the app tailors what it notices and what it asks you about. Pro users get a weekly summary generated by an AI model, running on anonymized pattern data rather than your raw photos or identity. Our privacy post explains exactly what leaves your phone and what doesn't.

The app is free to use. A Pro tier at $2.99 a month unlocks the weekly insight and longer history. That's the whole business model. No ads, no data sales, no surprise upsells.

What we'll never do

This matters more than the feature list, so I want to be specific.

We will never sell your data. Not to advertisers, not to insurance companies, not to data brokers, not to "research partners" with a vague privacy policy. The business model is subscriptions. Full stop. If that ever changes, you'll hear it from me first, directly, before it changes.

We will never claim Urivia can diagnose disease. The app helps you track color patterns and notice changes over time. That is a real, useful thing. It is not the same thing as a medical test, and pretending otherwise would both break FDA rules and hurt the people we're trying to help. If your pattern suggests something worth a doctor's attention, the app will say so and tell you to go see one. It will never tell you what you have.

We will never use dark patterns to lock you into Pro. The free tier is genuinely useful on its own. The paid tier exists because the AI summaries cost us money to generate and we need to cover that cost. If you cancel, your data stays yours and basic tracking keeps working.

We will never make you the product. You're the customer. The subscription is the whole deal.

What's next

The short-term roadmap is straightforward. Better accuracy on edge cases (very pale and very dark samples are harder than the middle of the range). Apple Health integration for people who want their hydration signals in one place. More condition-specific guidance for GLP-1 users, people managing chronic kidney disease, and people with type 2 diabetes.

The longer-term question is whether we expand beyond color into other at-home signals. The honest answer is: only if we can do it with the same standard we've held color to. If we can't build something genuinely accurate and genuinely private, we won't ship it just because a roadmap said we should.

I still read feedback personally. If something in the app feels wrong, or you think we got something wrong in a blog post, the support email goes to me. That won't scale forever, but it's how it works right now, and I like it that way.

When to see a doctor

A blog post can't examine you. If you're noticing any of the following, don't wait on an app. Call your doctor or go to urgent care.

Blood in your urine, or urine that's pink, red, or the color of cola. Severe pain when urinating. Fever combined with back or side pain. Sudden swelling in your legs, feet, or around your eyes. A noticeable drop in how much you're urinating. Confusion, dizziness, or extreme fatigue alongside dark urine. Persistent heavy foam or bubbles that don't go away after flushing, especially combined with any of the above.

Urivia is built to help you notice patterns over time. It is not built to handle emergencies, and it will tell you when something in your reading suggests you should talk to a clinician sooner rather than later.

How to track this yourself

The most useful thing you can do for your own hydration and kidney health is build a simple habit. Look at your urine in the morning, notice the color, and pay attention when it shifts for more than a day or two. Apps like Urivia let you log color patterns over time, which tends to be more useful than trying to remember whether last Tuesday looked the same as today.

If you'd rather not use an app, a note in your phone works. The tool matters less than the habit. What you're looking for isn't a single reading, it's the trend.

Frequently asked questions

Who built Urivia?

I did. It's a small, independent company, started after a personal moment that made the problem obvious: I saw something in my own bathroom I couldn't identify, and there was no good way to get an answer on my phone. There's no giant health conglomerate behind the app, which is both the reason it exists and the reason I can make promises about data and business model that larger companies can't.

Is Urivia a medical device?

No. Urivia is a general wellness app. It helps you track and notice patterns in urine color over time. It does not diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease, and it is not a substitute for medical testing or a conversation with your doctor. The language inside the app is designed carefully to stay within that lane.

How does Urivia make money?

Subscriptions. The free tier is genuinely free and genuinely useful. Pro is $2.99 a month and unlocks the weekly AI summary and longer history. We don't run ads, sell data, or take money from pharmaceutical companies or insurers. If that ever changes, I owe you a direct explanation first.

What happens to my photos?

Your photos are processed to extract a color reading and then handled according to the privacy settings you control. Our privacy post has the full breakdown of what's stored, where, and for how long. The short version: we store as little as we can get away with, and you can delete everything with one tap.

Can Urivia replace a urinalysis at my doctor?

No, and it isn't trying to. A clinical urinalysis measures things a phone camera physically cannot see, including protein, glucose, bacteria, crystals, and cells. Urivia tracks the color signal, which is one useful layer of a bigger picture. If your doctor has asked for a urinalysis, get the urinalysis.

Why should I trust you?

Honestly, you shouldn't yet. Trust is earned over time, and we've been in people's hands for a short window compared to companies with decades of track record. What I can offer in the meantime is clarity. The business model is subscriptions. The data policies are written plainly. The support email reaches me personally. Use the app for a few weeks, see if it holds up, and decide from there.
